Y'all. I need to tell on myself.

So you know the whole origin story of this blog? Someone asked my daughter what her dad does and she said "he's a nerd clown." I thought it was so good I named an entire website after it. Adorable right? Kid sees dad's job clearly. Great anecdote. I've been [telling it to everyone](https://www.linkedin.com/feed/update/urn:li:activity:7430292254625398784/).

Well. I showed my daughter the blog and she read it and she looked at me and said:

**"That's what Mom said."**

My *wife* called me a nerd clown. My daughter heard it and repeated it. And my brain, doing what brains apparently do, just... filed it under the wrong person. I didn't steal the idea on purpose. I didn't think "ah yes, let me take credit for my wife's joke." I genuinely believed my daughter came up with it. But the result is the same: a woman's idea got misattributed, and I didn't even notice until someone corrected me.

That really sat with me.

## This Isn't Just a Nerd Clown Problem

Here's the thing. Women's ideas getting absorbed, misremembered, or just quietly disappearing? It happens *everywhere*. In meetings. In classrooms. In pitch rooms. At kitchen tables, apparently.

There's actually a term for a specific version of this: **[hepeating](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/hepeat)**. That's when a woman suggests an idea and it gets ignored, then a man says the same thing and everyone loves it. A portmanteau of "he" and "repeat" was coined in 2017 by friends of astronomer Nicole Gugliucci and went viral because every woman on earth went "yep." My situation isn't exactly that. I didn't repeat my wife's line to get credit in a meeting. But it rhymes. It smells familiar. Whoever smelt it... 

The mechanism underneath is the same: a woman says a thing, and somehow a man ends up associated with it.

And that pattern? It's way bigger than one silly blog name.

## Read This Book

Joanne Lipman, former editor-in-chief of USA Today, wrote [That's What She Said: What Men and Women Need To Know About Working Together](https://www.amazon.com/Thats-What-She-Said-Together/dp/0062437224) and it is a genuinely important read. I'm not just saying that. The World Economic Forum made it their inaugural book club pick. Adam Grant called it essential reading for "good guys."

<img src="/blog/thats-what-she-said-cover.jpg" alt="Cover of That's What She Said by Joanne Lipman" style="float: right; margin: 0 0 1rem 1.5rem; max-width: 200px; border-radius: 8px; border: 2px solid #333;" />

What I appreciate about Lipman's approach is that it's not about shaming dudes. It's about showing how deep this stuff runs and what we can actually *do* about it. She mixes real data with real stories and the numbers alone will make you pause:

- **95%** of Fortune 500 CEOs are men
- Women hold only **17%** of seats on corporate boards
- Companies with more women in senior leadership **perform better** by virtually every financial measure
- Women employees boost creativity and temper risky behavior (like the financial gambles behind the 2008 economic collapse)
- Men routinely overestimate their abilities while women underestimate theirs

She digs into unconscious bias, how men and women communicate differently, even how the way we raise kids feeds into all of this. It's the kind of book that makes you go "oh. OH!" about twelve times per chapter. It made me rethink a bunch of stuff I thought I already understood.

Seriously. [Go read it.](https://www.amazon.com/Thats-What-She-Said-Together/dp/0062437224)

## It's Not Just a Feeling

And it's not just Lipman's book. [Pew Research Center](https://www.pewresearch.org/social-trends/2018/01/09/women-and-men-in-stem-often-at-odds-over-workplace-equity/) surveyed thousands of people about workplace discrimination and the numbers are hard to look away from:

<img src="/blog/hepeating-infographic.jpg" alt="Infographic showing percentage of women who experienced gender discrimination at work: 50% in STEM, 74% in computer jobs, 78% in male-dominated workplaces. Source: Pew Research Center, 2018" style="display: block; margin: 1.5rem auto; max-width: 500px; border-radius: 8px; border: 2px solid #333;" />

**50%** of women in STEM jobs say they've experienced gender discrimination at work. In computer jobs? **74%**. And for women working in male-dominated environments, it's **78%**. That's not a minority experience. That's most women in these spaces saying yeah, this happens to me.

The stats from Lipman's book are staggering on their own. But what got me is how this plays out in the most mundane, low-stakes moments too. I wasn't trying to climb a corporate ladder by stealing my wife's joke. I was telling a cute family story. And I *still* got it wrong. That's what makes this so tricky: it's not always malicious. Sometimes it's just how our brains work -- and that means you have to actively work against it.

Some stuff I'm trying to do now:

- **Credit the source** — If someone said a thing, say who said it. Every time. It costs nothing.
- **Amplify** — When someone's idea gets glossed over, bring it back. "I think what she said is really important."
- **Question your memory** — Mine is apparently not as reliable as I thought. Maybe yours isn't either.
- **Read && Listen** — I'm serious. Read this book. Talk about it. Listen.

## The Meta Part

So here's the thing. The origin story needs a correction. My wife called me a nerd clown. My daughter, being an excellent and hilarious human, repeated it when someone asked what her dad does. And I heard the retelling and credited the wrong person for a while. Made posts about it even.

The blog name stays. The name is perfect. But credit where it's due.

**My wife named this blog.**

It's kind of poetic that the origin story of Nerd Clown is itself a tiny case study in the exact thing Joanne Lipman wrote a whole book about. Women's ideas getting quietly reassigned. Not through malice. Just through not paying enough attention.

If you're a dude reading this: sit with it for a minute. Not to beat yourself up. But to notice. Lipman's whole thesis is that awareness is the first step, and men and women working *together* on this stuff is how it actually gets better. The good news is that noticing is something you can start doing today. Right now.

Now if you'll excuse me, I need to go thank my wife. For the blog name and for the patience.
